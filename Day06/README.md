Day06 – Phishing Email Analysis
Objective of the internship : 

To analyze phishing email samples, identify red flags, understand psychological manipulation techniques, assess potential risks, and suggest preventive measures.

Sample 1: “Dubai World Expo Fund Transfer” email (Google Forms)
1️. Identified red flags:

Too Good to Be True Offer
Promises €47,745,533 with 30% commission.
Generic Greeting (“Hello”)
No personalization or recipient name mentioned.
Suspicious Email Domain
Reply email: reem.alhashimi@kakao.com
Legitimate officials would not use free email services.
Emotional & Authority-Based Claim
Claims to be a Minister but provides no verifiable credentials.
Grammatical Errors & Poor Sentence Structure
Example: “Am a women and serving as a Minister…”
Unusual Financial Arrangement
Asking a stranger to receive millions on their behalf.
Use of Google Forms Branding
Attempt to appear legitimate using a trusted platform.

2️. Why this email is suspicious I felt?

This email is a classic advance fee / business partnership scam.
It attempts to involve the recipient in transferring a large sum of money under false pretenses.
No legitimate government official would:
Randomly email strangers
Offer 30% of millions
Use public email services
Ask for financial partnership without verification

3️.Psychological manipulation used : 

Greed appeal – 30% of €47 million triggers financial temptation.
Authority bias – claims to be a minister.
Scarcity /exclusivity – implies you were specially selected.
Confidential tone – makes it feel like a secret opportunity.

4️.Possible impact if someone responds:

Financial fraud (advance processing fees)
Identity theft (sharing bank details/passport)
Money laundering involvement
Legal consequences
Long-term targeting for more scams

5️.Prevention steps:

Never trust unexpected financial offers.
Verify sender domain carefully.
Do not share bank or identity details.
Report such emails as phishing.
Enable spam filtering and email security controls (SPF, DKIM, DMARC awareness).

Sample 2: “American Express Account Suspension” Email
1️.Identified red flags:

Urgency language
“Review your information now”
Threat-based wording
“Temporary suspension until you verify”
Suspicious sender domain
administraciones@pentagon-seguridad.cl
Not an official American Express domain.
Clickable button link
“Click here to review your account now”
Likely leads to phishing page.
Generic message
No account-specific details (last 4 digits, etc.).
High importance flag
Used to create panic.

2️.Why this email is suspicious?

The sender domain does not match the official American Express domain.
Legitimate financial institutions use verified corporate domains.
The email pushes immediate action through a clickable button — a common phishing tactic.

3️.Psychological manipulation used:

Fear – account suspension threat.
Urgency – immediate action required.
Authority – uses American Express branding.
Loss aversion – fear of losing access to funds.

4️.Possible impact if someone clicks the link:

Credential harvesting (username/password)
Banking information theft
Credit card fraud
Malware download
Account takeover

->General prevention measures:

Always verify sender domain carefully.
Hover over links before clicking.
Access accounts directly through official websites.
Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
Conduct phishing awareness training.
Use email security gateways with anti-phishing detection.

Conclusion of today's task:
Both emails use psychological manipulation, urgency, and impersonation to trick users into revealing sensitive information.

Phishing attacks succeed not because of technical complexity, but because they exploit human psychology.

Understanding red flags and thinking critically before clicking is the strongest defense.
